Tips

Skin-to-skin contact between you and your baby is important. It promotes bonding and helps regulate baby’s temperature, breathing and heart rate after birth. It also helps the baby “suck” at the breast and begin to feed.

Ensure correct position and attachment. Your breasts are still soft for few days after the birth, then as breast milk changes from highly nutritious colostrum to mature milk, your breasts can become quite full and ﬁrm. Try and use the first few days to get your position and attachment right, this may help to avoid potential problems down the track.

Be patient. Like anything new, breastfeeding is a skill that takes time and patience to learn. Relaxation is important for both you and your baby. If you find you are getting frustrated or angry at yourself while you're trying to breastfeed, stop and try again in a little while.

Feed on demand, even if it’s very frequent. Every time you breastfeed, your body gets the signal to start producing more milk. The more frequent, the more often your body will produce.

Hold off on dummy toys like pacifiers. Especially at the beginning, your baby is learning to breastfeed and being given a dummy to suck on can cause confusion and interfere with the breastfeeding process.
